From Field to Fabric

How Natural Fibers Are Made

Every natural fiber follows a unique journey from plant or animal to finished textile. Understanding this process helps you make informed, conscious choices.

Overview

The Fiber Journey

Natural fibers begin as raw agricultural materials — cotton bolls on a plant, flax stems in a field, fleece on a sheep, or cocoons spun by silkworms. From there, each fiber travels through three broad stages: harvesting, processing, and finishing. The methods used at each stage determine the fiber's quality, environmental impact, and suitability for different products.

Stage 1

Harvesting

Harvesting is the first step in the fiber journey — collecting the raw material from its natural source. The method of harvesting varies dramatically by fiber type and has a significant impact on both quality and environmental footprint.

🤲

Hand Harvesting

Traditional method for cotton, flax, and silk. Selective picking preserves fiber quality but is labor-intensive and costly.

🚜

Mechanical Harvesting

Machine harvesting is faster and cheaper but can damage longer fibers, reducing quality and increasing waste.

✂️

Shearing

Wool is harvested by shearing sheep annually. Skilled shearers can remove the fleece in one piece, preserving its value.

⚠ What to Watch For

Mechanically harvested fibers often contain more short, broken fibers which require additional chemical processing to bind together. Hand-harvested fibers (especially organic cotton and linen) generally produce stronger, longer-lasting textiles.

Stage 2a

Mechanical Processing

After harvesting, raw fibers must be cleaned, separated, and aligned before they can be spun into yarn. Mechanical processing uses physical methods — no chemicals — to prepare the fibers.

🌀

Ginning

Cotton gins separate fibers from seeds. Roller gins preserve longer fibers; saw gins are faster but more damaging.

💧

Retting

Flax and hemp stems are retted (soaked) to separate bast fibers. Dew retting is eco-friendly; water retting is faster but pollutes.

🪮

Carding & Combing

Fibers are brushed into alignment. Carding produces softer, airier yarns. Combing removes short fibers for smoother, stronger yarn.

🧵

Spinning

Aligned fibers are twisted into yarn. Ring spinning is traditional and strong. Open-end spinning is faster but produces weaker yarn.

✅ The Gold Standard

Mechanically processed fibers — especially those using roller ginning, dew retting, and ring spinning — produce the highest quality textiles with minimal chemical intervention. Look for terms like "ring-spun", "roller-ginned", and "dew-retted" on product labels.

Stage 2b

Chemical Processing

Many conventional textiles undergo chemical treatments to alter their properties, appearance, or performance. While these processes can enhance certain qualities, they often come with environmental and health trade-offs.

🧪

Scouring

Hot water and detergents remove natural oils, waxes, and impurities. Alkaline scouring is common but harsh on fibers and waterways.

☁️

Bleaching

Hydrogen peroxide or chlorine bleach whiten fibers for dyeing. Chlorine bleach produces toxic byproducts; peroxide is safer.

🎨

Dyeing

Synthetic dyes are cheap and vibrant but often contain heavy metals and require toxic mordants. Natural dyes are safer but less colorfast.

🛡️

Finishing Treatments

Wrinkle-resistance, water-repellency, and flame retardants are achieved through chemical finishes — many contain formaldehyde or PFAS.

⚠ Chemical Risks

PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) are used for water and stain resistance. Known as "forever chemicals," they persist in the environment and accumulate in the human body. Azo dyes can release carcinogenic amines. Formaldehyde is used in wrinkle-free finishes and can cause skin irritation.

Look for: GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard) certification, OEKO-TEX Standard 100 labels, and brands that disclose their chemical processing methods. Avoid "wrinkle-free," "permanent press," and "stain-resistant" claims on synthetic-treated garments.

Stage 3

Finishing

The finishing stage transforms raw woven or knitted fabric into the final product. Finishing techniques range from purely mechanical processes to heavy chemical treatments — and this is where many hidden health and environmental risks are introduced.

🔄

Mechanical Finishing

Brushing, sanding, and calendaring alter fabric texture without chemicals. These are the safest, most eco-friendly finishing methods.

🧴

Wrinkle-Resistant

Often achieved with formaldehyde-based resins. Can cause skin allergies. Look for "naturally wrinkle-resistant" fibers like linen or hemp.

💧

Water-Repellent

DWR (Durable Water Repellent) finishes often contain PFAS. Safer alternatives include beeswax coatings and silicone-based finishes.

✅ What to Look For

The safest finished textiles use minimal chemical treatments and are transparent about their finishing processes. Look for:

  • GOTS-certified organic fibers — no toxic finishes allowed
  • OEKO-TEX Standard 100 — tested for harmful substances
  • MADE IN GREEN — transparent, traceable production
  • Brands that explicitly state "no chemical finishes" or "PFAS-free"
  • Naturally wrinkle-resistant fibers: linen, hemp, and tightly woven cotton
